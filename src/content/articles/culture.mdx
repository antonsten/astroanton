<p>I was recently talking with a potential client about a position that sounded interesting. Like most of the work I do, it would mean <a href="https://www.antonsten.com/uxlead/">leading the UX</a> and shaping the team's objectives and goals. Additionally though, it would be similar to my teaching at design schools or my newly launched <a href="https://www.antonsten.com/course">"Pick my brain"</a> - the sharing of knowledge to help a team reach a higher goal.</p>
<p>The discussions were going great and I was truly excited about the project. Being a client that has one of their offices nearby from where I live (and have my office), I realized that the discussion of remote or on-location would surface. He had already mentioned that they had a couple of designers at another office too, so I figured working remotely wouldn't be an issue with weekly or bi-weekly office checkins. But as discussions were about to finish up, he said that <em>"as such an important part of the work is to help form the culture, he strongly believes that I should be on-location at least 4 days / week".</em></p>
<p>I was a bit amazed to be honest. I admit that there are benefits to working in the same location, but I was baffled that people still so strongly believe that physical presence is needed in order to perform work.</p>
<p>The discussions have left me wondering why this struck a chord inside of me and what I believe.</p>

<p>But if shaping a culture would mean you need to be in the same office, wouldn't that mean that these teams would be completely without culture?</p>
<h2 id="what-culture-is">What culture is</h2>
<p>Well, first of all, I think we need to state the obvious. Regardless of what you do and where you are, you're going to have <em>a</em> culture. It might not be a <em>great</em> one, but it's sort of like physical appearance. Regardless if you workout, wear make-up, or eat junk food you're going to have one. It will surely differ depending on your choices, but either way, you're going to have one. Culture is just like that.</p>
<p>I recently started a new project with <a href="https://superfriendlydesign.systems/?ref=antonsten.com">SuperFriendly</a> and it's my first experience <em>directing</em> a project with them. I was fortunate enough to get to work with <a href="https://twitter.com/jessiofhall?ref=antonsten.com">Jessi Hall</a> again, who also produced our project for <a href="https://www.antonsten.com/case/toast/">Toast</a> last fall, but also have <a href="https://twitter.com/SaraSoueidan?ref=antonsten.com">SƒÅra SoueidƒÅn</a> (who I've followed from a distance for a long time!) and <a href="https://twitter.com/sazpeitia?ref=antonsten.com">Sarah Azpeitia</a> on my team. So if culture requires someone to be on-location, how would I be able to help shape a culture for a team of four? We're not just in different countries and timezones, but actually on four different continents!</p>
<p>In the back of my mind, I've been processing what I think the foundation of a great culture is and how that tends to lead to at least <em>better</em> project results (and sometimes <em>amazing</em> results).</p>
